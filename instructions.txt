--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. SYSTEM OVERVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

StrideSync measures bilateral running gait using 6 IMU sensors and 4 force
sensitive resistors worn on both legs. Data is processed on an ESP32
microcontroller and can be:

  - Logged to a microSD card during outdoor runs, then downloaded over WiFi
  - Streamed live over WiFi UDP during treadmill runs (planned — Mode 4)

A Python dashboard on your laptop loads the data and produces:
  - 3D bilateral skeleton animation (hip, knee, ankle, toe)
  - Joint angle time series (knee flexion, ankle dorsiflexion, L vs R)
  - Gait metrics (cadence, ground contact time, tibial shock, symmetry index)
  - Fatigue comparison (first 10% vs last 10% of run)
  - Garmin cross-validation (cadence, ground contact time agreement %)

Channel mapping:
  Ch0 = L-Foot    Ch1 = L-Shank    Ch2 = L-Thigh
  Ch3 = R-Foot    Ch4 = R-Shank    Ch5 = R-Thigh

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. HARDWARE SETUP
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

MICROCONTROLLER
  ESP32 DevKit V1 (38-pin)
  I2C: SDA=GPIO21, SCL=GPIO22
  FSR ADC: GPIO32 (L-heel), GPIO33 (L-fore), GPIO34 (R-heel), GPIO35 (R-fore)
  Haptic motor: GPIO25
  SD card SPI: CLK=GPIO18, MISO=GPIO19, MOSI=GPIO23, CS=GPIO5

IMU SENSORS
  6x MPU-6050 (GY-521) via PCA9548A multiplexer (I2C address 0x70)
  All IMUs share address 0x68 — mux isolates each channel
  I2C clock: 100kHz (do NOT increase — validated stable at this speed)
  Pull-ups: 5kΩ external resistors on SDA and SCL (internal pull-ups disabled)

FORCE SENSITIVE RESISTORS
  4x FSR-402 in voltage divider (10kΩ to GND)
  Higher pressure = higher ADC reading (0–4095)
  Placement: left heel, left forefoot, right heel, right forefoot

SD CARD
  SanDisk 16GB Class 10 microSD in SPI module
  Wired to GPIO 18/19/23/5 as above
  FAT32 formatted (standard)

POWER
  3.7V 1000mAh LiPo per unit, TP4056 charging module, SPDT power switch
  Estimated runtime: ~5 hours per charge

CALIBRATION (every boot)
  Place all IMUs flat and still when the 3-pulse haptic fires.
  Hold still for ~5 seconds per IMU until the 2-long-pulse confirmation.
  Offsets stored in RAM — redo on every power cycle.

3. SENSOR PLACEMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

3a. GENERAL PRINCIPLES

  Goal: measure the orientation of each body segment as accurately as possible
  with minimal movement of the sensor relative to the underlying bone (soft
  tissue artifact). Every placement decision below is driven by this principle.

  Rules of thumb:
    - Prefer bony surfaces over muscle bellies — less sensor wobble
    - Orient sensors consistently across sessions so data is comparable
    - Strap firmly: the sensor should not rotate or slide during a heel strike
    - Calibrate with sensors flat on a table BEFORE strapping on (see 3h)
    - Mark your skin or garment so the position is repeatable across sessions

  Anatomical reference diagram (right leg, lateral view):

       [Hip / Greater Trochanter]
               |
         Ch5: R-Thigh IMU         ← lateral midpoint of thigh
               |
        [Lateral Knee]
               |
         Ch4: R-Shank IMU         ← 3–5 cm below lateral joint line
               |
        [Lateral Malleolus]
               |
         Ch3: R-Foot IMU          ← dorsal midfoot (3rd metatarsal shaft)
               |
           [Toes]

  FSR-404 sensors are inside the shoe — see sections 3e and 3f.


3b. THIGH IMU  (Channel 2 = Left, Channel 5 = Right)

  Anatomical location:
    Lateral aspect of the thigh, at the MIDPOINT between the greater trochanter
    and the lateral femoral condyle.

  How to find the landmarks:
    - Greater trochanter: the bony bump at the top of the outer hip. Palpate
      laterally while rotating the leg — it moves under your fingers.
    - Lateral femoral condyle: the bony prominence just above the outer knee.
    - Midpoint: halfway between these two — roughly mid-thigh laterally.

  Why here:
    - Mid-lateral thigh has the least soft tissue movement relative to the femur.
    - Anterior surface (over rectus femoris) and posterior surface (hamstrings)
      have large muscle belly excursions during running — avoid these.
    - The midpoint avoids the proximal and distal muscle bulk.

  Orientation:
    - Long axis of the GY-521 board aligned approximately with the long axis
      of the femur (vertical when standing).
    - Board face (component side) pointing away from the body (laterally).

  Securing:
    - Wide compression sleeve or neoprene wrap is strongly preferred.
    - Thigh is the hardest segment to keep stable — use widest strap available.
    - If taping, use 5–7 cm wide elastic athletic tape (e.g. Leukotape K)
      wrapped once around the thigh, covering the sensor completely.
    - Test by jogging 50m: if the sensor rotates or slides distally, re-secure
      before the run.


3c. SHANK IMU  (Channel 1 = Left, Channel 4 = Right)

  Anatomical location:
    Lateral aspect of the lower leg, on the flat surface of the tibia,
    approximately 3–5 cm below the lateral tibial plateau (outer knee joint line).

  How to find the landmark:
    - Lateral tibial plateau: palpate just below the outer knee — a flat shelf
      of bone before the fibular head. Place the sensor just distal to this.
    - The proximal 1/3 of the tibia has a relatively flat lateral surface between
      the bony crest anteriorly and the peroneal muscle group posteriorly.

  Why here:
    - The proximal tibia is subcutaneous — minimal soft tissue between skin and
      bone means low movement artifact during impact.
    - This location captures tibial shock (high-frequency impact transient that
      propagates up the tibia at heel strike) before it is attenuated by soft
      tissue. This is the standard clinical placement for tibial shock studies.
    - Proximal placement also gives a cleaner signal for shank angular velocity.

  Why NOT the medial tibia:
    - The medial border is sharp and uncomfortable for running. Lateral is fine.
    - Avoid the anterior tibial crest — it is prominent and cable routing there
      is uncomfortable.

  Orientation:
    - Long axis of board aligned with the long axis of the tibia (vertical).
    - Board face pointing laterally (away from body).

  Securing:
    - Neoprene sleeve with a sewn pocket is ideal.
    - Athletic Velcro strap 3–4 cm wide, cinched firmly around the upper shin.
    - Do NOT place directly on the anterior tibial crest — always on the flat
      lateral surface.


3d. FOOT IMU  (Channel 0 = Left, Channel 3 = Right)

  Anatomical location:
    Dorsal (top) surface of the foot, over the dorsal midfoot — approximately
    over the shaft of the 3rd metatarsal or the navicular bone.

  How to find the landmark:
    - Run your finger along the top of the foot from the ankle toward the toes.
    - The navicular is the prominent bone on the medial-dorsal arch.
    - The 3rd metatarsal shaft runs from mid-foot toward the middle toe.
    - Target: midline of the dorsal foot, at approximately 40–45% of foot
      length from the heel.

  Why here:
    - The midfoot is the most rigid part of the foot during running — low
      intra-foot movement means the sensor tracks foot segment orientation well.
    - Avoid the hindfoot (heel): the FSR is there, and the calcaneus moves
      relative to the midfoot during pronation.
    - Avoid the forefoot (metatarsal heads / toes): high deformation during
      push-off causes large artifact.
    - Dorsal placement ensures the sensor is not compressed underfoot.

  Orientation:
    - Long axis of board aligned with long axis of foot (heel to toe).
    - Board face pointing upward (away from ground) when foot is flat.

  Securing:
    - Two strips of 2.5 cm medical tape (Leukotape P or Cover-Roll Stretch)
      crossed over the sensor, anchored to the shoe upper or the foot.
    - A small neoprene pouch on the shoe upper (sewn or glued) with a hook-and-
      loop closure is more secure for longer runs.
    - Do NOT place inside the shoe — the sensor will be compressed underfoot.


3e. HEEL FSR  (GPIO 32 = Left, GPIO 34 = Right)

  Anatomical location:
    Under the posterior plantar surface of the calcaneus (heel bone), slightly
    medial of centre — the point of maximum loading at heel strike.

  Placement:
    - Place the FSR disc inside the shoe, between the factory insole and the
      shoe upper (under the insole).
    - Position: in the heel cup, centred approximately 1–2 cm anterior to the
      very posterior edge of the insole, slightly medial.
    - The lead wires exit from the medial or posterior edge of the heel cup and
      route up the medial or lateral side of the shoe.

  Why this location:
    - During heel strike in a rearfoot runner, peak plantar pressure occurs
      under the posteromedial heel. This is where the FSR has highest sensitivity.
    - For midfoot/forefoot runners, the heel FSR will show lower amplitude —
      this is expected and biomechanically correct.

  Securing:
    - Under the insole provides natural compression hold.
    - If the FSR slides, a small dab of shoe-safe adhesive or double-sided tape
      on the non-sensing face will hold it in place.


3f. FOREFOOT FSR  (GPIO 33 = Left, GPIO 35 = Right)

  Anatomical location:
    Under the 2nd–3rd metatarsal heads, at the ball of the foot.

  Placement:
    - Inside the shoe, under the insole, at approximately 30% of insole length
      from the toe edge.
    - Centre the FSR disc over the 2nd–3rd metatarsal heads (the middle of the
      ball of the foot, not the big toe side or little toe side).
    - Lead wires route medially or laterally, same direction as heel FSR wire.

  Why here:
    - Peak propulsive plantar pressure during push-off occurs at the 2nd–3rd
      metatarsal heads in most runners.
    - The timing difference between heel FSR and forefoot FSR defines the
      loading pattern: heel → fore indicates heel striker; simultaneous or fore
      first indicates midfoot/forefoot striker.


3g. SECURING METHODS (summary by segment)

  Foot:
    Medical tape (Leukotape P or Cover-Roll Stretch) over the sensor, anchored
    to the shoe upper. Or a neoprene pouch sewn onto the shoe. Re-tape each
    session. Expected: very stable.

  Shank:
    Neoprene compression sleeve with a sewn lateral pocket. Alternatively: 3 cm
    Velcro strap, tightened firmly. Test by grabbing and rotating the sensor —
    it should not move. Expected: stable if strap is tight enough.

  Thigh:
    5–7 cm wide elastic athletic tape (Leukotape K / Elastikon) wrapped fully
    around the thigh. Or a thigh compression sleeve with sewn pocket. This is
    the most prone to migration — tape all the way around, not just across the
    sensor. Expected: some drift over long runs without a proper sleeve.

  FSRs:
    Under insole with optional double-sided tape on non-sensing face. The
    insole provides enough compression in normal shoe fit. Expected: very stable.

  Wire routing:
    Route all IMU cables along the lateral leg (outside of leg, not medial
    between thighs). Secure cable every 10–15 cm with medical tape to prevent
    tugging. Leave a small loop of slack at each joint to accommodate joint
    flexion without tension. The star topology (all wires run to a central
    hub at the waist or knee level) prevents inter-segment tugging.


3h. CALIBRATION NOTE FOR WEARABLE USE

  The Mahony filter calibrates by assuming the sensor Z-axis points upward
  during calibration (gravity detected on Z). The current firmware does this
  on every boot.

  For prototype breadboard use: hold each IMU flat on a table during calibration.

  For the wearable (sensors sewn into garment):
    1. Power on the ESP32 BEFORE strapping the device on.
    2. Lay the garment completely flat on a table, all 6 sensors face-up
       (component side up, Z-axis pointing toward ceiling).
    3. Wait for the "3 short + 5 slow" calibration haptic sequence to complete
       for all 6 sensors (~30 seconds total).
    4. THEN strap the garment onto your legs and run.

  Do NOT calibrate while wearing the device — the sensors will not be
  horizontal and the gravity reference will be wrong, producing incorrect
  roll/pitch angles for the entire session.

  Future improvement: implement an in-situ calibration mode that uses the
  known standing posture (leg vertical) to establish segment reference frames
  without requiring the sensors to be physically horizontal.

4. FIRMWARE MODES  (previously section 3)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Change the mode by editing this line in ESP32/src/main.cpp:

  #define MODE_SELECT <number>

Then re-upload via PlatformIO (Ctrl+Alt+U).

  Mode  1  Raw IMU output — all channels, labelled (debugging)
  Mode  2  FSR test — reads and prints ADC values
  Mode  3  Mahony filter — roll/pitch/yaw on all 6 IMUs (development/validation)
  Mode  4  WiFi UDP streaming — PLANNED (not yet implemented)
  Mode  5  SD card binary logging at 200Hz — USE FOR OUTDOOR RUNS
  Mode  6  Haptic feedback test
  Mode  7  Gait event detection — PLANNED
  Mode  8  Bilateral synchronisation — PLANNED
  Mode  9  Full functionality — PLANNED
  Mode 10  Raw plotter — comma-separated output for Serial Plotter
  Mode 11  WiFi file server — download SD logs to laptop after a run

HAPTIC FEEDBACK MEANING
  3 short pulses   — calibration starting, place IMU flat
  5 slow pulses    — hold still during calibration
  2 long pulses    — calibration complete
  3 short pulses   — Mode 5/11 ready (SD and/or WiFi connected)
  Motor on solid   — file download in progress (Mode 11)
  5 quick pulses   — file download complete (Mode 11)

5. RUNNING A SESSION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

5a. TREADMILL / INDOOR RUN — WiFi streaming (PLANNED — not yet implemented)
    Mode 4 will stream UDP packets to a Python receiver on your laptop in
    real time. When implemented, the workflow will be:
      1. Set MODE_SELECT 4, upload firmware
      2. Boot ESP32, allow calibration to complete
      3. Run WiFi receiver script on laptop (to be built)
      4. Run on treadmill
      5. Stop receiver — CSV is written automatically
      6. Load CSV in dashboard

5b. OUTDOOR RUN — SD card logging (IMPLEMENTED)

    BEFORE THE RUN
    1. Format microSD as FAT32, insert into SPI module
    2. Set MODE_SELECT 5, re-upload firmware in PlatformIO
    3. Power on ESP32
    4. Wait for calibration sequence:
         - 3 short pulses: place IMUs flat and still
         - 5 slow pulses during each IMU calibration (~5s each, 6 IMUs total)
         - 2 long pulses per IMU: calibration done
         - 3 short pulses: SD ready, logging started
    5. Serial monitor shows the log filename (e.g. run_001.bin)
    6. Strap device to legs and begin run

    DURING THE RUN
    - Data logs continuously at 200Hz to the SD card
    - SD card is flushed every 2 seconds to minimise data loss
    - If power is cut mid-run, the last ~2s of data may be lost

    AFTER THE RUN
    - Power cycle the ESP32 (do NOT remove SD card while powered)
    - Proceed to section 5 to transfer data

6. TRANSFERRING DATA TO THE LAPTOP
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

STEP 1 — SWITCH TO FILE SERVER MODE
  Edit main.cpp:
    #define WIFI_SSID  "your_network_name"
    #define WIFI_PASS  "your_password"
    #define MODE_SELECT 11
  Re-upload firmware. Boot ESP32. The IP address prints to the Serial monitor:
    "Connected. IP: 192.168.1.42"
    "  http://192.168.1.42/files"
  3 short haptic pulses confirm the server is ready.

STEP 2 — RUN THE FETCH SCRIPT ON YOUR LAPTOP
  Open a terminal in the Dashboard folder:

    cd Stridesynch/Dashboard

  Install dependencies if not already done:
    pip install -r requirements.txt

  List available log files:
    python transfer/fetch_log.py --ip 192.168.1.42 --list

  Download and convert the latest log:
    python transfer/fetch_log.py --ip 192.168.1.42

  Download a specific file:
    python transfer/fetch_log.py --ip 192.168.1.42 --file run_003.bin

  Save to a specific folder:
    python transfer/fetch_log.py --ip 192.168.1.42 --out runs/2025-06-01/

  Delete a file from the SD card after downloading:
    python transfer/fetch_log.py --ip 192.168.1.42 --file run_001.bin
    python transfer/fetch_log.py --ip 192.168.1.42 --delete run_001.bin

STEP 3 — OUTPUT
  The script downloads the .bin file, converts it to .csv, and prints:
    "Saved CSV: run_001.csv"
    "Load this file in the StrideSync dashboard: /path/to/run_001.csv"
  The .bin file is deleted automatically (pass --keep-bin to retain it).

ALTERNATIVE — PHYSICAL SD CARD TRANSFER
  If WiFi is unavailable:
    1. Power off the ESP32
    2. Remove the microSD card
    3. Insert into laptop card reader
    4. Copy run_XXX.bin to your Dashboard folder
    5. Convert manually:
         python -c "
         from data_loader import load_binary
         df = load_binary('run_001.bin')
         df.to_csv('run_001.csv', index=False)
         print(len(df), 'records written')
         "
    6. Load the resulting CSV in the dashboard

7. DASHBOARD USAGE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

START THE DASHBOARD
  cd Stridesynch/Dashboard
  pip install -r requirements.txt   (first time only)
  python app.py
  Open browser: http://localhost:8050

TEST WITH SAMPLE DATA (no hardware needed)
  cd Stridesynch/Dashboard/sample_data
  python generate_sample.py 30       # generates 30-second synthetic run
  cd ..
  python app.py
  Enter "sample_data/sample_run.csv" in the Run CSV field, click Load Run.

LOADING A REAL RUN
  1. Enter the full path to your run CSV in the "Run CSV" field
     e.g.  C:/Users/Noah/runs/run_001.csv
  2. Click "Load Run"
  3. Wait ~3 seconds for the skeleton animation to compute (750 frames)
  4. All three panels populate automatically

PANEL 1 — 3D SKELETON ANIMATION
  Shows hip, knee, ankle, and toe positions for both legs simultaneously.
  - Blue = left leg, Orange = right leg
  - Hips are at the top (Y=0), toes at the bottom
  - Click Play to animate at 0.5× real-time speed
  - Drag the scrub bar to jump to any point in the run
  - Rotate/zoom the 3D view by clicking and dragging
  - Y axis = vertical, Z axis = sagittal (forward/back swing)

PANEL 2 — JOINT ANGLES
  Time series plots of:
  - Knee flexion (°): 0° = full extension, 60°+ = peak swing flexion
  - Ankle dorsiflexion (°): positive = dorsiflexion, negative = plantarflexion
  Left and right are overlaid for direct bilateral comparison.
  Hover for exact values. Zoom with scroll wheel. Double-click to reset.

PANEL 3 — GAIT METRICS
  See section 8 for definitions of each metric.
  For Garmin comparison: enter the path to a .fit file exported from
  Garmin Connect in the "Garmin .fit" field before clicking Load Run.

8. GARMIN COMPARISON (VALIDATION)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PURPOSE
  Cross-validate StrideSync cadence and ground contact time against the
  Garmin HRM-Pro / Forerunner metrics from the same run.

EXPORT A FIT FILE FROM GARMIN CONNECT
  1. Open Garmin Connect (app or web)
  2. Select the activity
  3. Settings → Export Original → saves as .fit file

LOAD IN DASHBOARD
  Enter the .fit file path in the "Garmin .fit (optional)" field BEFORE
  clicking Load Run. The metrics panel will show:
    Cadence:              Garmin: XXX spm | StrideSync: XXX spm | Agreement: XX%
    Ground Contact Time:  Garmin: XXX ms  | StrideSync: XXX ms  | Agreement: XX%

  Agreement ≥ 95% (shown in green) is considered validated.
  Agreement < 95% (shown in amber) indicates calibration or placement issues.

NOTE ON GARMIN CADENCE FIELD
  Garmin's 'cadence' field is steps per minute for one foot.
  StrideSync reports bilateral steps per minute (both feet).
  The comparison script multiplies Garmin cadence × 2 for correct comparison.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
9. GAIT METRICS EXPLAINED
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

CADENCE (spm)
  Steps per minute. Computed from heel strike interval on each FSR.
  Typical distance running: 160–180 spm. Higher is generally more efficient.

GROUND CONTACT TIME (ms)
  Time from heel strike to toe-off per step.
  Typical: 200–280 ms. Shorter is generally better.
  Asymmetry in GCT between feet can indicate compensation for injury.

TIBIAL SHOCK (g)
  Peak resultant acceleration at the shank IMU (proximal tibia).
  Correlates with impact loading rate. Typical running: 5–12g.
  Values above 10g may indicate excessive heel striking or hard landings.

SYMMETRY INDEX (%)
  |left − right| / ((left + right) / 2) × 100
  Applied to knee angle, ankle angle, and cadence.
  0% = perfect bilateral symmetry. <5% is considered normal for healthy runners.
  Asymmetry >10% warrants investigation (injury, fatigue, compensation).

FATIGUE COMPARISON
  Mean knee flexion angle over the first 10% vs last 10% of the run.
  A positive delta (last > first) indicates increasing knee flexion with fatigue,
  consistent with reduced leg stiffness and neuromuscular fatigue.
  Delta >2° (shown in red) is flagged as notable.

10. TROUBLESHOOTING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

"No IMUs found — check wiring"
  - Verify SDA/SCL wired to GPIO 21/22
  - Check 5kΩ pull-up resistors are installed on SDA and SCL
  - Verify PCA9548A address pins A0/A1/A2 are tied to GND (address 0x70)
  - Power cycle ESP32 and check connections

"SD init failed — check wiring"
  - Verify SD SPI wiring: CLK=18, MISO=19, MOSI=23, CS=5
  - Confirm card is FAT32 formatted
  - Try a different SD card (some brands have compatibility issues)

"Could not reach [IP] — is the ESP32 in Mode 11?"
  - Confirm WIFI_SSID and WIFI_PASS are correct in main.cpp
  - Confirm laptop and ESP32 are on the same WiFi network
  - Check Serial monitor for the actual IP address
  - Try pinging the IP from a terminal: ping 192.168.1.42

"Load error" in dashboard
  - Confirm the CSV has a header row and 20 columns
  - Run the file through the converter again to check for corruption
  - Check that the binary file size is a multiple of 70 bytes

"Yaw is drifting during the run"
  - Expected behaviour — no magnetometer fitted
  - Yaw is not used in any joint angle or metric calculation
  - Roll and pitch capture all clinically relevant gait kinematics

"Knee angle stuck near 0°"
  - Ensure the two adjacent IMUs are not loose on the segment
  - Check that calibration was done with IMUs in their mounted position
  - If enabling Ki in the Mahony filter, start at 0.01 and validate first

"Joint angles look wrong or drifting immediately"
  - Most likely the sensors were not horizontal during calibration
  - Recalibrate with the garment laid flat on a table, sensors face-up
  - See section 3h for the correct wearable calibration procedure

"Tibial shock reads very low (<3g) during running"
  - Shank IMU may have slipped distally toward the ankle
  - Re-secure at the proximal 1/3 location (3–5 cm below lateral knee)
  - Ensure strap is tight enough to prevent rotation at heel strike

"FSR reads pressure on the wrong foot / wrong phase"
  - Double-check GPIO wiring: L-heel=32, L-fore=33, R-heel=34, R-fore=35
  - Confirm FSR is actually under the correct foot in the shoe
